MIRA PHOTO ANALYZER — SENIOR TECH REVIEW
Real-world test: 9 factory photos, live bot
Date: 2026-03-17
Reviewer notes written for Mike

======================================================================
HOW THIS TEST WORKED
======================================================================
Nine real factory photos — P&IDs, wiring diagrams, ladder logic,
one-line diagrams, panel schedules — were sent to the MIRA bot
through Telegram, same as you would on the job. Each photo got the
same caption: "Please identify this industrial equipment. Note the
make, model, and any issues visible." Bot replies were collected
and evaluated below.

======================================================================
OVERALL VERDICT
======================================================================

GRADE: Second Opinion — but only for diagram identification.
Not ready to rely on for fault diagnosis.

Here's the honest picture:

What it does well:
The bot correctly figured out what TYPE of drawing it was looking
at every time — P&ID, wiring diagram, ladder logic, one-line,
panel schedule. It never confused a panel schedule for a one-line
or a P&ID for ladder logic. For a tech who just wants to quickly
sort through a pile of unfamiliar plant prints, that's genuinely
useful. You'd save time.

Where it falls short:
Identifying the drawing type is the easy part. What a senior tech
actually needs is: what equipment is this, what are the tag numbers,
what does the fault path look like, is there anything obviously
wrong in this drawing? MIRA's current answers stop at "here's a
list of text I could read" and then ask you what you want to know.
That puts the work back on you.

The three specific problems that need fixing before shop-floor use:

1. The JSON bleed on Photo 7. When a tech sees curly braces and
   backtick-json in a reply, they assume the tool is broken — and
   they're not wrong. This one bug alone would cause most techs
   to stop trusting the tool.

2. Sparse reads on Photos 1, 4, and 9. Three out of nine photos
   came back essentially empty. On a real job, a 1-in-3 miss rate
   is not acceptable. These might be fixable with better photo
   technique (better lighting, no screen glare, cropped closeups),
   but the bot currently gives no guidance on that.

3. Screen-vs-drawing confusion on Photo 5. If a tech is looking
   at ladder logic on a laptop screen and takes a photo, the bot
   reads the software interface instead of the logic. That's a
   real workflow problem since most techs ARE looking at screens.

Bottom line:
Use it as a quick first-pass to identify diagram types and pull
tag numbers when the photo is clean. Don't use it to confirm a
diagnosis or make a call on equipment without following up with
additional questions or a manual check. It's a decent research
assistant if you know its limits — not yet a tool you'd hand to
a new tech and tell them to trust.

Fix the JSON leak first. That's the one that will kill adoption
fastest if techs see it in the field.
