## What MIRA is

MIRA is a maintenance-diagnostic AI co-pilot for industrial plants. A tech opens it on their phone, snaps a photo of the broken equipment or types a fault code, and gets a cited answer pulled from the customer's own OEM manuals and prior fault history.

One sentence (homepage hero): *Diagnose a fault in 30 seconds, not 30 minutes.*

One sentence (technical): *MIRA reads your manuals so your techs don't have to — every answer cites the source page.*

## Who it's for

**Primary buyer:** SMB plant managers, maintenance supervisors, MRO buyers at facilities with $5M–$200M annual revenue. 35–55 years old. Has been sold bad CMMS software at least once before. Skeptical of slick demos, allergic to "AI-powered" copy.

**Primary user:** maintenance technicians on the floor. 25–55 years old. Phone in pocket, hands often in equipment. Reads on a 6" screen during shift, on a laptop between work orders. They are domain experts — fluent in fault codes, amp draw, PM schedules. Marketing register loses them in 3 seconds.

**Secondary buyer:** reliability engineers, plant engineers, ops managers at the same facilities. Care about MTBF, OEE, and root cause analysis.

## ICP pain points (ranked by frequency in interviews)

1. **Manual hunt.** A fault hits at 2am. The tech is paging through an 800-page PDF on a 7" tablet. The OEM 800-number says 24-hour callback. The line is down. Every minute = $X (industry-specific, but always real).
2. **Knowledge attrition.** The retiring senior tech knows where everything's buried; the apprentice doesn't. PMs get rescheduled because nobody knows the right interval. Tribal knowledge isn't documented.
3. **CMMS distrust.** Bought UpKeep / MaintainX / Fiix / Limble / IBM Maximo. Spent 6 weeks importing, never finished onboarding the techs, the system became a write-only work-order form. Now the data is fragmented across 3 places.
4. **Tool sprawl.** Manuals on Google Drive. Work orders in CMMS. Fault history in someone's notebook. Photos on the supervisor's phone. Nothing talks.
5. **Vendor opacity.** Vendor said the new $200K drive would "just work." It didn't. Now in month 4 of finger-pointing between OEM, integrator, and the in-house electrician.

## The 4 psychological pillars (the things to hit hardest in promos)

1. **Speed under pressure.** When the line is down, every second is dollars. MIRA answers in seconds, with a cited source so the tech knows why to trust it.
2. **Their stuff, not generic.** MIRA works on the manuals YOU upload. Your equipment, your specs, your fault history. Not a generic AI guessing.
3. **The tech keeps their dignity.** The product is a peer, not a teacher. It doesn't condescend, doesn't over-explain, doesn't lecture about what a fault code is.
4. **No 6-week onboarding.** Drop in a manual, ask a question, get an answer. If the first answer doesn't help, you haven't lost a quarter on rollout.

## Tone of voice (verbatim instructions for any narration)

- Confident, not enthusiastic. State facts, let the listener draw conclusions.
- Trade vocabulary used naturally, never explained. (If you're explaining what "amp draw" means, you've lost the audience.)
- Numbers, not adjectives. "11 seconds" beats "fast." "25,000 manuals" beats "comprehensive."
- Named, not abstract. "Allen-Bradley PowerFlex 525" beats "your VFD." "Mike H., maintenance supervisor" beats "our customers."
- Speaks as a peer, not a vendor. "Your techs are hunting through 800-page PDFs" beats "We help your team."

## What MIRA is NOT

- Not a CMMS replacement (it can integrate with the one you have, or operate without one)
- Not a predictive-maintenance vibration-sensor product (different category — Tractian, Augury)
- Not a vendor-locked OEM tool (works on any OEM's manuals you upload)
- Not an "AI assistant" in the generic ChatGPT-wrapper sense (domain-tuned, source-cited)
