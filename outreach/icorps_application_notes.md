# NSF I-Corps — Application Notes

## What is I-Corps?

NSF Innovation Corps is a 7-week entrepreneurial training program that teaches researchers and founders to validate commercial potential through structured customer discovery interviews. The program was developed at Stanford and is now administered through a national network of universities.

The core methodology is simple: before building more, go talk to 30+ potential customers and figure out if your commercial hypothesis is actually true.

## Why I-Corps Matters for MIRA

- **Required** for the NSF STTR Fast-Track pathway ($1.55M) — you cannot access Fast-Track funding without I-Corps completion
- Validates commercial assumptions with real data before Phase II investment
- The 30 customer interviews are taken seriously by NSF reviewers — they want to see that you've tested the market
- Introduces you to a network of mentors, fellow founders, and potential customers
- Gives the university research partner academic credibility for the commercial translation work

## Team Requirements

I-Corps requires a team of 2-3:

| Role | Person | Requirement |
|------|--------|-------------|
| Entrepreneurial Lead (EL) | Mike Harper | Must be from the small business, commits to conducting the interviews |
| Technical Lead (TL) | University Co-PI (Dr. Torres or Dr. Ruiz) | Must be a researcher — usually the university PI |
| I-Corps Mentor | Assigned by program | Experienced entrepreneur or investor, assigned by I-Corps site |

The EL (you) does most of the work — attending weekly cohort sessions and conducting 3-5 customer interviews per week for 7 weeks.

## Application Requirements

1. Technology with a plausible commercial application (working prototype strengthens this significantly)
2. 1-2 page application: describe the technology, the problem it solves, and your initial commercial hypothesis
3. Commitment from both EL and TL to complete the 7-week program
4. Both people available for weekly cohort sessions (in-person or hybrid, depending on site)

## Commercial Hypothesis to Test

**Primary hypothesis:**
"Industrial maintenance technicians at manufacturing plants with 50-500 employees will pay $500-2,000/month for an AI diagnostic co-pilot delivered through their existing messaging platform, because it reduces diagnostic time by 25-40% and fills the knowledge gap created by retiring senior technicians."

**Secondary hypotheses to test in parallel:**

1. *Who actually has budget authority?* Is it the maintenance manager, plant manager, or operations director?
2. *What is the real pain?* Diagnostic time? Or something else — compliance documentation, insurance audits, training cost?
3. *What is the real barrier to adoption?* IT security approval? Union agreements? Technician trust in AI?
4. *Is the messaging platform delivery actually an advantage?* Or do plant IT departments block WhatsApp/Telegram?
5. *Who are the competitive alternatives?* Existing CMMS? Vendor hotlines? Tribal knowledge? Do they see a "tool" problem at all?

## Customer Discovery Plan (30+ interviews)

The interviews are not demos or sales calls. The goal is to understand the customer's world — not to pitch MIRA. Ask about their current process, their frustrations, their workarounds. Only describe MIRA at the very end to get a reaction.

| Segment | Target Count | Why This Segment |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Maintenance managers at mfg plants | 10 | Budget authority, understands the cost problem |
| Field service / maintenance technicians | 10 | The actual users — do they want AI help? |
| Plant managers / operations directors | 5 | Final sign-off, care about uptime and labor cost |
| Equipment OEM service departments | 5 | Potential partners or competitors — how do they diagnose remotely? |
| System integrators (Rockwell/ABB/Siemens partners) | 5 | Channel partners — do they have customers who need this? |

## Interview Script Framework

*Note: This is a framework, not a script. Good interviews are conversations.*

**Opening (establish context):**
- "Tell me about your current role and how long you've been in it."
- "Walk me through what happens when a piece of equipment goes down unexpectedly."

**The problem (let them describe it, don't lead):**
- "What's the hardest part of diagnosing an unfamiliar fault?"
- "How do you usually figure out what's wrong with equipment you haven't seen before?"
- "What would it cost your plant if that process took twice as long as it does now?"

**Current workarounds:**
- "What tools do you use today to help with diagnostics?"
- "Have you tried any AI or software tools for maintenance support? What happened?"

**Closing (if relevant, not mandatory):**
- "If you had a tool that could help a junior tech diagnose any fault by texting a photo, what would that be worth to you?"

## What to Do With Results

After 30 interviews, you should be able to answer:

1. Is the pain real enough that plants would pay to solve it?
2. Who specifically pays — and what is their budget process?
3. What features matter most vs. what was assumed?
4. What is the biggest adoption barrier?
5. What should Phase I validate to de-risk Phase II investment?

These answers go directly into the NSF STTR Project Description and Commercialization Plan.
